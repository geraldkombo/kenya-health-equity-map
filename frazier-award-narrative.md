## Narrative Application

### Problem Statement

In Kenya, a woman's chance of surviving childbirth depends almost entirely on where she lives. In Nairobi, over 80% of births are attended by a skilled health professional. In Turkana, that figure drops below 30%. The gap is not merely statistical — it represents thousands of preventable maternal deaths each year.

The barriers are not single but compound: long travel distances to the nearest facility with a skilled birth attendant, high poverty rates that make transport costs prohibitive, and low facility density that forces women to deliver at home without skilled care. Yet until now, these barriers have existed in separate datasets — a travel time study here, a poverty survey there, a facility list somewhere else — inaccessible to the community health teams and county planners who need them most.

### Our Solution

The Kenya Health Equity Map (KHEM) is an open-source, privacy-preserving digital platform that integrates travel time, poverty, facility density, and **skilled birth attendance (SBA)** data into a single, publicly accessible map of all 47 Kenyan counties. Its core innovation is the **Priority Gap Score (PGS)** — a composite index (0–100) that identifies the most underserved counties at a glance.

For maternal health specifically, the platform now surfaces SBA rates from the Kenya Demographic and Health Survey (KDHS 2022) at the county level, alongside travel time estimates from WHO AccessMod. This allows users to:

1. **Identify maternal health access deserts** — counties where low SBA (<50%) coincides with high travel time (>60 min) and high poverty (>40%), indicating compounded barriers to safe delivery.
2. **Compare neighboring counties** side-by-side to benchmark resource allocation and advocate for equitable distribution of maternal health funding.
3. **Generate printable advocacy briefs** that community health teams and County Health Management Teams (CHMTs) carry directly into planning meetings to negotiate for mobile maternal clinics, ambulance services, and skilled birth attendant deployment.

### Community Engagement

The tool has been workshopped with community health volunteers in **Turkana, Tana River, and Elgeyo Marakwet** — three counties with among the lowest SBA rates nationally. During these workshops, community members validated the data against local knowledge and identified over a dozen previously unmapped health facilities for submission to OpenStreetMap. This community-validated approach ensures the platform reflects ground truth, not just official records.

### Impact to Date

- **1,699 community-mapped facilities** serving as the baseline for accessibility analysis
- **47 county profiles** with integrated maternal health indicators
- **3 community workshops** validating data accuracy and identifying unmapped facilities
- **Open-source codebase** (MIT license) enabling replication in other LMIC contexts

### How Award Funding Would Be Used

The Kenneth C. Frazier Award would enable three critical expansions:

1. **Maternal Health Overlay Module** — Add a dedicated "Maternal Health Access" view to the map that layers SBA rates, travel time to the nearest facility with maternity services, and poverty data. This would allow users to toggle between general infrastructure disparities and maternal-specific access barriers with one click.

2. **Community Training Expansion** — Extend workshops to 5 additional high-priority counties (Mandera, Wajir, Marsabit, Samburu, West Pokot) where SBA rates are critically low (<35%). Each workshop trains CHVs and HFMCs to use the platform in CHMT planning meetings.

3. **Mobile-Responsive Offline Mode** — Optimize the platform for the low-bandwidth environments where maternal mortality is highest. The existing service worker caches map tiles and data; award funding would enable field testing and refinement of the offline experience in rural clinics without reliable internet.

### Alignment with Award Criteria

The platform exemplifies the Frazier Award's commitment to equity by making spatial data accessible to the communities most affected by maternal health disparities. It is:
- **Community-centered:** Designed with and validated by CHVs in underserved counties
- **Data-driven:** Uses open, verified data from KNBS, KDHS, and OSM
- **Open and replicable:** Fully open-source under MIT license
- **Privacy-preserving:** No tracking, no login, no cookies
- **Action-oriented:** Generates printable briefs for direct use in advocacy and planning
